Question 2.
Jabberwockies sleep for around 7 hours a night, on average. Perform an appropriate statistical test to determine whether the mean sleep_total in the sampled population of animal types differs from the typical value for jabberwockies.

obs_stat_sleep_total <- msleep %>% 
  summarise(mean_sleep = round(mean(sleep_total), 2))
obs_stat_sleep_total
# The mean sleep total for all animals = 10.43 hours

# Does the mean DIFFER from the population sample mean - two tailed as it's not
# asking if it's more or less.

# H0: There is no difference between the mean hours of sleep for jabberwockies
# and the population.
# H1: The mean hours of sleep for the population differs from that of the 
# jabberwockies.

# create a null distribution
# 7 is the average hours of sleep for the jabberwockie (mu = hypothesis mean
#  and we're hypothesizing that the population mean is the same)
null_distribution_total_sleep <- msleep %>% 
  specify(response = sleep_total) %>% 
  hypothesise(null = "point", mu = 7) %>% 
  generate(reps = 50000, type = "bootstrap") %>% 
  calculate(stat = "mean")
null_distribution_total_sleep
Response: sleep_total (numeric)
Null Hypothesis: point
 

# Visualise. The red line will be our observed stat i.e. the mean for the 
# population.
# The bars will be the means of means.
null_distribution_total_sleep %>% 
  visualise(bins = 30) +
  shade_p_value(obs_stat = obs_stat_sleep_total, direction = "both")


# p value 
p_value_sleep_total <- null_distribution_total_sleep %>% 
  get_p_value(obs_stat = obs_stat_sleep_total$mean_sleep, direction = "both")
Warning: Please be cautious in reporting a p-value of 0. This result is an approximation based on the number of `reps` chosen in the `generate()` step. See `?get_p_value()` for more information.
p_value_sleep_total

# p = 0.
# As p < 0.05, we can reject our null hypothesis in favour of the alternative
# hypotheseis - the mean hours of sleep for the population differs from that of 
# the jabberwockies.

Question 4. Perform an appropriate statistical test to determine whether the proportion of domesticated animal types in the population of animal types is greater than 5% Hint Think about creating an is_domesticated variable for the analysis

# one tailed test (domesticated greater than 5%) and PROPORTION not mean

# H0: Domesticated animals do not make up more than 5% of the population 
# H1: Domesticated animals are more than 5% of the population

# Create is_domesticated column 
# There were: True = 10, False = 44, NA = 29
# Should I take out the NAs? Having looked at them, I'd say it's very unlikely 
# many of them have been domesticated, so I haven't... 
domesticated_or_not <- msleep %>% 
  mutate(conservation = replace_na(conservation, "no")) %>% 
  mutate(is_domesticated = conservation == "domesticated", .after = 5)
domesticated_or_not

domesticated_or_not %>% 
  group_by(is_domesticated) %>% 
  summarise(count = n())
# Now 73 false and 10 true.

# Looking at the proportion of domesticated animals
domesticated_or_not %>%
  group_by(is_domesticated) %>%
  summarise(prop = n()/nrow(domesticated_or_not)) %>%
  filter(is_domesticated == TRUE)
# 0.12 = 12% which is of course higher than 5% but we want to do a statistical

Question 1.
You work for a independent coffee shop. The boss tells you that she thinks that around \(40\%\) of people in the town (population \(30,000\)) know of the coffee shop, but you are skeptical of the estimate. You conduct a random survey of \(200\) people in the town, asking if respondents are aware of your coffee shop. You want to use the sample to test the hypothesis that \(40\%\) or more of the towns population have heard of the coffee shop.


Question 2.
You work for a website design company and have performed an A/B test on the position of a banner on a website promoting a particular item.

A/B testing A method comparing two versions of a web page, email, flyer or other marketing device against each other to determine which version performs better. As it is essentially a controlled experiment, the design should try to ensure that the groups experiencing both versions of the marketing device are equivalent and representative of the population.



You selected five days at random last month and then randomly selected \(200\) of each sampled days users into group \(A\) and another \(200\) of that days users into group \(B\). Group \(A\) continued to be shown the banner at the right hand side of the webpage (its usual position) while group \(B\) was shown the banner at the top of the page. You monitored each sampled users interactions with the website, in particular the click through rate (CTR) on the banner, i.e.what proportion of sampled users clicked on the banner. You want to use the sampled data to test the hypothesis that website users overall are more likely to click on the banner if positioned at the top of the page

Question 3.
You work as an analyst for a car manufacturing company - they have specific standards they must meet for standards and regulation purposes. You have been asked to check the quality control of the manufacture of a particular car part. You have been given data on a sample of \(200\) parts produced over the period of a week (the sampled parts were pulled at random from the production line and measured: the factory produced a lot more than \(200\) parts that week). The specification of the part stipulates a width of \(145\textrm{mm}\), and the manufacturing process is said to have drifted if the mean width of parts differs significantly from \(145\textrm{mm}\). You want to use the sampled measurements to test whether the process overall has drifted.
